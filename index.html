Problem Statement

Through the transitioning phase between the traditional media and internet, my understanding of science, technology, and history came from regulated television channels: National Geographic, Discovery Channel, History TV. These were channels with editorial standards, fact-checkers, and a commitment to accuracy.

Today, social media has democratized content creation. Anyone with a smartphone can reach millions of people; no required training, no ethical code, no consequences for spreading falsehoods. The result? Truth and misinformation exist side by side, with no clear line between them.

The Concerning Parts


Fake News Spreads Faster

A landmark study analysing X data found that false news stories spread significantly farther, faster, and more broadly than true stories, reaching 1,500 people. 6 times faster than the truth (Vosoughi, Roy & Aral, 2018).

Trust Is Collapsing

The Edelman Trust Barometer (2023) shows a consistent, alarming decline in trust toward traditional journalism. When people stop trusting mainstream sources, they turn to alternatives. Today, these are unregulated social media accounts.

No Fact Checking

Recent research found that two-thirds of influencers do not fact-check their content before posting. They are trusted voices with millions of viewers and they still do not create content responsibly.

Why We Believe Misinformation

Think about your own social media scrolling. How many posts do you see? Dozens? Hundreds? According to researcher Eli Pariser (2011), we experience a "filter bubble". Our feeds are personalised, isolating us from opposing viewpoints. Consuming sheer volume of information leaves us with no mental energy to evaluate every post, so our brains take shortcuts.

Hasher, Goldstein, and Toppino (1977) discovered that people are more likely to believe a statement is true simply because they have been exposed to it before. This is the "illusory truth effect." If you see a claim repeated enough times, even if it's false, your brain starts to accept it. This is why misinformation campaigns repeat the same falsehoods over and over.

On social media, we often use likes, shares, and comments as a shortcut for credibility. Metzger, Flanagin, and Medders (2010) found that people instinctively think: "This post has thousands of likes, it must be trustworthy." But virality has no connection to truth. Emotional, dramatic, and outrageous content gets more engagement, regardless of its accuracy.

We are hardwired to seek, interpret, and remember information that matches what we already believe. Nickerson (1998) explains this as confirmation bias. On social media, most of us don't bother to research or verify ideas that challenge our existing views. We scroll past them or dismiss them, creating echo chambers where misinformation thrives.

A 2024 study by Wiegmann and colleagues revealed that when a source had a verified credential like a blue checkmark indicating "Medical Doctor", users perceived their non-expert posts as more credible. It’s the "virtual lab coat effect." It proves that simple identity verification can be misleading. We need signals about the content itself, not just the creator's identity.

How Misinformation Spreads – The Platform Problem

Algorithmic Bias for Engagement

Vosoughi, Roy, and Aral (2018) found that false news spreads faster precisely because it evokes stronger emotions i-e surprise, outrage, fear. These emotional reactions drive engagement, which algorithms amplify further.

Echo Chambers & Filter Bubbles

Pariser (2011) coined "filter bubble" to describe how algorithms personalise our feeds. Sunstein (2017) added "echo chambers". Closed environments where existing beliefs are constantly reinforced, making us resistant to outside facts.

Influencers as Opinion Leaders

Katz and Lazarsfeld's (1955) Two-Step Flow theory explains that ideas flow through trusted "opinion leaders." On social media, influencers fill this role when they share misinformation, their followers trust it, making it appear credible.

Why This Is a Big Problem for Society

Keyes (2004) defines “post-truth” as a culture where public opinion is shaped more by emotion and personal belief than by objective facts. This environment nourishes misinformation. When facts become secondary to feelings, any claim can gain traction.

Uscinski and Parent (2014) showed that conspiracy theories often frame credible sources like government agencies or mainstream media as liars. This makes users resistant to fact-checking from official sources. If you believe “they” are lying to you, why would you trust a correction from “them”?

Kahan (2017) discovered that people subconsciously reject valid information if accepting it would threaten their identity or values. For example, someone might accept that cigarettes are harmful but reject evidence about sugar’s hazards because it conflicts with their lifestyle.

Benkler, Faris, and Roberts (2018) analysed how disinformation networks frame their messaging around grievances. “The system is rigged against us,” “they don’t want you to know this.” When people believe they are being oppressed, fact-checking feels like an attack, not a correction.

Wineburg and McGrew (2019) found a critical difference between fact-checkers and regular users. Fact-checkers use “lateral reading”, they open new tabs to investigate a source’s credibility. Non-experts use “vertical reading”, they stay on the original site and are easily fooled by professional-looking design. Most users lack fact-checker skills. They need tools to help them.

The Trust Signals

Imagine walking through a busy market. Stalls everywhere, vendorsshouting that their product is the best. How do you decide who to trust? You look for signalslikea long-standing reputation, a recommendation from a friend, a certification from a recognized authority.

Social media is like that market and The Trust Signals are designed to provide those signals.

What Are Trust Signals?

A system built to provide visual heuristics for credibility and transparency, so the users can make informed, confident decisions about the content they consume.

A visual badge system that a content creator earns by following specific ethical practices in their content creation process, providing an at a glance signal for its authenticity.

Why Visual Badges?

Speed

Users process visuals 60,000 times faster than text. A badge communicates trust in a split second without even reading a single word.

Universality

Badges transcend language barriers. A well-designed icon communicates meaning across cultures without requiring translation.

Consistency

Over time, users learn what each badge means, creating a shared vocabulary for credibility that scales across all popular social media platforms.

The Three Badges

COLLECTED

What it means: The creator engaged with primary, real-world, or grassroots information.

How it's earned:

  • Interviews local experts or eyewitnesses
  • Gathers on-the-ground data
  • Surveys real communities

Why it matters: Fights "context collapse" (boyd, 2010). Signals that content is grounded in a verifiable reality.

CREDITED

What it means: The creator respects intellectual labor and traces ideas to their origin.

How it's earned:

  • Attributes ideas properly
  • Credits footage and images
  • Links to original inspiration

Why it matters: Restores expert authority. Makes it harder for misinformation to hide behind anonymity.

CURATED

What it means: The creator is transparent about editing, compilation, and AI use.

How it's earned:

  • Labels AI-generated sections
  • Discloses composite edits
  • Explains the curation process

Why it matters: Addresses deepfakes and synthetic media. Makes honesty a competitive advantage.

Sustainability & Ethics

I have completed NTU’s ethical framework and BAFTA Albert sustainable practices within this project. I’m also following GDPR and IP protocols to make sure everyone is safe from legal and social issue.

Ethical Framework

Legal
GDPR-compliant data handling; all participants anonymous with right to withdraw; intellectual property respected; creator contracts used.
Social
Inclusive design, badges are free to use without any monetization in mind. Diverse representation in questionnaire outreach and equal opportunity towards hard working creators.
Ecological
Digital-first distribution; minimal energy use in production; no physical waste. Website hosted on green-energy-powered servers where possible.
Economical
Non-monetary incentive model: badges are earned through ethical practice, not purchased. Potential platform partnerships ensure long-term sustainability.

Sustainability

The Trust Signal is designed to be self-sustaining. All digital resources can be maintained with minimal ongoing resources. Future funding may come from platform partnerships or institutional grants, but the core system remains free initially.

Ethics

My questionnaires include clear consent from creators and users. Creators are offered a pledge to be responsible on their own. Occasional checks will be carried out to make sure the honesty is maintained. I reflect on my own self to remain critically self-aware.
